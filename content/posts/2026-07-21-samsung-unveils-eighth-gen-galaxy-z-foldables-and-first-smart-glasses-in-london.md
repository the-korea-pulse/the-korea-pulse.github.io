Samsung Electronics is staging its next Unpacked in London on the night of July 22 Korea time, using the venue to introduce the eighth generation of its Galaxy Z foldable line alongside the company's first pair of smart glasses. The lineup, headlined by the Galaxy Z Fold 8, arrives as Samsung looks to its mobile hardware to carry momentum through the back half of the year.

## A Foldable Line Now Eight Generations Deep

The Galaxy Z franchise has reached its eighth iteration, a run that has made Samsung the most visible name in the still-niche foldable category it helped create. Bringing the Fold 8 to a London stage rather than a home-market launch underscores how central overseas markets have become to the series, where Samsung faces a widening field of foldable rivals.

Foldables remain a premium, relatively low-volume slice of the smartphone market, which sharpens the stakes for each refresh. A generational update is where Samsung defends pricing power at the top of its range and signals whether the form factor can keep drawing upgraders.

## Stepping Into Wearable Displays

The bigger departure this year is hardware rather than a folding phone: Samsung is introducing its first smart glasses. The move pushes the company into head-worn computing, a category that major consumer-electronics players have circled for years without a clear mass-market winner. Pairing the debut with an established foldable launch gives the new device an immediate audience and lets Samsung frame glasses as part of a connected device family rather than a standalone experiment.

## The Second-Half Question

Domestic coverage has framed the launch around a pointed question: can a strong hardware cycle help pull Samsung's non-memory business back toward growth in the second half? That remains an open question rather than a settled outcome. Foldable and wearable volumes feed component demand across Samsung's own divisions, but the direct earnings impact will only become clear once shipment and pricing data land in the months ahead.

## Company in Context

The launch fits a long pattern of reinvention for a group that began in 1938 as a trading firm founded by Lee Byung-chul and pushed into electronics near the end of the 1960s. That electronics arm has since become the company's defining business and helped make Samsung the fifth-most valuable brand worldwide as of 2024. Each Unpacked is, in effect, a test of whether that brand can keep commanding a premium as the devices behind it grow more experimental — this year, quite literally, by putting a display on your face.
