Samsung took the wraps off its next generation of foldable phones on the 22nd, local time, at Old Billingsgate in London, using the Galaxy Unpacked 2026 stage to launch the Galaxy Z Fold8 and a broadened Galaxy Z lineup pitched squarely at younger users. The headline change is a physical one: when the new Fold is opened, the crease that has defined foldable screens for years has all but disappeared, leaving a display surface that reads as flat to the eye and smooth to the touch.

## A Flatter Fold, and Why It Matters

The most immediate takeaway from the Fold8 is what you can no longer feel. Earlier foldables carried a visible seam down the middle of the inner screen, a reminder of the hinge underneath. On the new model, that ridge has been engineered down to the point where running a finger across the unfolded panel no longer catches on it. For a category that has spent several years fighting the perception that its central innovation was also its central flaw, erasing the crease is less a spec-sheet footnote than a statement about whether foldables are ready for the mainstream.

## Courting the 2030 Generation

Alongside the hardware, Samsung is repositioning who the Galaxy Z line is for. The company framed the expanded lineup around younger buyers, leaning into content creation and lifestyle use rather than pure productivity. Widening the Z family — rather than treating the foldable as a single flagship halo device — signals a bid to move foldables from a niche premium tier toward a volume product with entry points at more than one price and form factor.

## Reworking the Unpacked Stage Itself

The launch also marked a shift in how Samsung stages these events. For the London show the company worked with Tony Award-winning creator Hue Park to rethink the presentation, treating Unpacked less as a straight product briefing and more as a designed cultural moment. Pairing a phone reveal with a Broadway-caliber creative collaborator underscores the same theme running through the products: Samsung wants the foldable line associated with culture and creativity, not just engineering.

## The Weight Behind the Launch

The company doing all this is not a newcomer betting the house on a single device. Samsung traces back to 1938, when Lee Byung-chul founded it as a trading firm in Taikyu — present-day Daegu — in Japanese-ruled Korea, and it moved into electronics near the end of the 1960s before branching into construction and shipbuilding through the mid-1970s. After the founder's death in 1987, the empire split into five separate groups, including Shinsegae, CJ, Hansol and JoongAng, alongside Samsung itself. Today it stands as South Korea's largest family-run chaebol, led by chairman Lee Jae-yong from the Samsung Town complex in Seoul, and it held the world's fifth most valuable brand ranking in 2024. That scale is the backdrop against which the Fold8 lands: a mature conglomerate using a flagship launch to argue that foldables have finally outgrown their compromises.

## What Comes Into Focus Next

The crease may be gone to the touch, but the real test is commercial. Samsung has now committed to a wider, younger-skewing foldable range and to a more theatrical launch strategy to sell it. Whether the flatter screen and the broadened lineup translate into the mainstream adoption the category has long promised is the question the coming quarters will answer.
